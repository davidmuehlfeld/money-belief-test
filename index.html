    Three generations, one belief

    Brad Klontz finished graduate school as a psychologist with $100,000 in student loan debt, right as the dot-com bubble peaked. His friends were making six figures trading tech stocks. So he sold everything he had and put all of it into tech stocks. Three months later the bubble burst.

    Within six months he had lost everything.

    The interesting part isn't the loss. It's what he did next: he went home and interviewed his own mother and father about money. What came out was a family history he had never been told. His grandfather lost everything in the Great Depression. Showed up at the bank one day and simply couldn't get in. Doors locked. Money gone.

    The belief he formed that day was: you can't trust banks. He kept his money in a lockbox in the attic and lived that way for the rest of his life. He died at 93 and never put a single cent in a bank. Klontz's mother improved on it slightly. She'd use a bank, but she would not touch the stock market.

    And Brad?

    Raised by people terrified of risk, he put 100% of his money into the most speculative asset he could find. Opposite behavior. Same unexamined script, just inverted. Once he understood the history he asked himself: fine, I made this mistake, I get why I did it. Now what can I do about it?

    That question is the whole point of this exercise. Otherwise he'd have swung straight back to his grandfather's belief. Never trust institutions. The script doesn't disappear when it burns you. It chooses a different disguise.

    His account is in the Hidden Brain episode “Rewrite Your Money Story” and in this interview. The three-generation version is told most fully here.

    The research

    The framework is the Klontz Money Script Inventory (KMSI), developed by financial psychologist Brad Klontz with Sonya Britt, Kristy Archuleta, and Ted Klontz, published in the Journal of Financial Therapy in 2011. It's free to read: newprairiepress.org/jft/vol2/iss1/1.

    What they did

    422 people rated their agreement with 72 money-related beliefs. Factor analysis reduced those to four distinct patterns: money avoidance, money worship, money status, money vigilance. Three of the four correlated significantly with income and net worth.

    What the four actually predict

    A follow-up study — How Clients' Money Scripts Predict Their Financial Behaviors (Klontz & Britt, Journal of Financial Planning, 2012) — tied the scripts to eight measurable disordered money behaviors, including compulsive buying, pathological gambling, compulsive hoarding, workaholism, financial dependence, financial enabling, financial denial, and financial enmeshment. Specific findings worth knowing:

    • Avoidance is negatively associated with trusting professional financial advice. If you're an Avoider and you just felt an urge to dismiss this whole section, that's the finding, working.
    • Worship is associated with revolving debt — people who worship money were likely to carry it — and with the belief that more money will make you happier.
    • Status is the self-worth/net-worth fusion, which locks people into competing with the people around them. Related research links being over-concerned with financial success and materialism to lower well-being, lower vitality, and higher anxiety.
    • Vigilance is the healthy one, and is negatively related to compulsive buying. The catch is that being watchful shades into being anxious.

    On the happiness question

    The original 2011 paper leans on the older view that wealth and happiness are barely related. That view has since been revised, and I'd rather tell you than quietly recycle it. Kahneman and Deaton's famous $75,000 plateau (2010) was challenged by Killingsworth (2021), who found no plateau at all. The two camps then ran an adversarial collaboration — Income and emotional well-being: A conflict resolved, PNAS 2023 — and the resolution is this: for most people, happiness keeps rising with income, with no ceiling. The flattening appears mainly among the least happy, and only above roughly $100,000. (Plain-English summary from Wharton.) So the anti-guru line “money doesn't buy happiness” is also a script. It's just a more socially acceptable one.

    Where the model is weak

    Be suspicious of anyone who sells you a personality test without this part. The original 422 respondents were recruited from a listserv of financial planners, coaches, and mental health providers — 82% Non-Hispanic White, 65% female. Not a general population. And in September 2025, a re-validation of the revised inventory (KMSI-R) using 2,686 respondents found that internal consistency held up (α = .863) but factorial validity did not — the four-factor model showed poor fit (CFI = .762, TLI = .742), and measurement invariance across racial and ethnic groups was not supported. Read it: Journal of Family and Economic Issues.

    The four types are a genuinely useful mirror. They are not a diagnosis, and the instrument they're built on needs revision. Use this to notice something about yourself. I'm not a financial adviser, and none of this is financial advice.
